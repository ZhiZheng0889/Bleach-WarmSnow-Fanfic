## Scene 1

- **POV**: Master Jian (Cloud-Splitting)
- **Location**: A temporary council room
- **Goal**: Keep the revolution from hardening into a new court

They built the council out of borrowed chairs.

That was the first good sign.

Nothing borrowed felt eternal.

But even borrowed chairs could become thrones if people sat in them long enough.

The room smelled like wet wood and ink.

The windows were half covered with cloth to keep the sun from turning the meeting into a spectacle.

People had learned, quickly, that sunlight made crowds.

Crowds made pressure.

Pressure made bad decisions feel like destiny.

Master Jian watched the way bodies arranged themselves.

Who spoke first.

Who waited to be asked.

Who sat near the door like they owned the exit.

Who kept their hands visible.

Who kept them hidden.

The arguments were already learning the old patterns.

Someone said, “We need a stamp.”

Someone else said, “Stamps are what the court used.”

A third voice—tired, practical—said, “Without a stamp, the depot can’t tell which lists are real.”

Master Jian listened.

He did not hate organization.

He hated the way organization disguised control.

At the side table, a clerk stacked paper.

Permits.

Stamps.

Rules.

Paper walls.

Somewhere beyond the city, Soul Society was learning the same lesson.

Steel intimidated.

Paper endured.

Master Jian remembered the basin and how quickly stone became a center.

Paper could become a center, too.

He leaned forward.

“If we build a new court out of forms,” he said, “then we have only changed the material, not the chain.”

Silence.

Not agreement.

Offense.

No one liked being told they were repeating history.

History, after all, was always someone else’s fault.

One council member—broad-shouldered, hands scarred from labor—glared at him.

“So what?” she said. “We do nothing? We let food rot because we’re afraid of paper?”

Master Jian nodded slowly.

“We do something,” he said. “But we do it in a way that does not make someone a permanent gate.”

Another member, younger, eyes bright with ideology, leaned forward.

“No gates,” he said. “No leaders. Everything is communal.”

The laborer snorted.

“Communal doesn’t haul crates,” she said. “People haul crates.”

Master Jian almost smiled.

That was the real argument.

Not paper versus freedom.

Work versus myth.

He tapped the table.

“Write your lists,” he said. “Keep records. Make decisions. But build in refusal.”

The young idealist frowned.

“Build in what?”

“Refusal,” Master Jian repeated. “Rotation. Transparency. A way to say ‘no’ to whoever starts acting like the basin.”

The room shifted.

Some people looked relieved.

Some looked threatened.

Threatened, Master Jian thought, was a useful sign.

It meant the idea had found the right nerve.

## Scene 2

- **POV**: Ren
- **Location**: Outside the council room
- **Goal**: Decide whether to step into governance or remain outside it

Ren stood outside the door and listened.

He heard words that were almost gentle.

Rotation.

Records.

Refusal.

He heard other words that made his skin crawl.

Stamp.

Permit.

Necessary.

His chest tightened.

Not with fear.

With recognition.

Forms were not so different from doors.

Both decided who passed.

Both pretended they were neutral.

Neutral was just a word people used when they didn’t want to name who benefited.

Ren had spent days trying not to become a gate.

But gates were going to be built anyway.

If he stayed outside, they would build them without him.

And one day, someone would decide the door between worlds should be managed.

Managed meant owned.

Owned meant controlled.

Controlled meant weaponized.

Ren exhaled.

He pressed his palm to the wood.

The seam inside him twitched at the idea of entry.

He pulled his hand back.

He would not open that kind of door.

Not with power.

With consent.

He knocked.

Inside, voices stopped.

Footsteps approached.

The door opened a crack.

Master Jian’s eyes met Ren’s.

Recognition flared—fast, careful.

Not possession.

Assessment.

Ren held his hands visible.

“I’m not here to take a chair,” Ren said.

Master Jian’s mouth twitched.

“Good,” he replied. “Then come in.”
