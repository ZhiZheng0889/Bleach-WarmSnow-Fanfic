# Chapter 12 — Petition for a Crown

## Scene 1

- **POV**: Lady Gao Yue
- **Location**: The palace approach (white stone steps)
- **Goal**: Force divine legitimacy; make the Spirit King the Compact’s anchor

Lady Gao Yue had avoided the palace for months.

Not out of reverence.

Out of strategy.

A king’s presence was a solvent.

It dissolved inconvenient politics into destiny.

But it also dissolved the nobles’ control.

If the Spirit King spoke, he might speak against them.

If he stayed silent, they could speak for him.

Both outcomes were useful.

But only one was safe.

Now, safety was a luxury the realm had burned.

Gao Yue climbed the steps with a procession behind her: House heads, priests, scribes bearing the Registry of Breath, and guards carrying a lacquer box she did not look at.

The lacquer box was escalation sealed in wood.

At the gate, palace wardens hesitated.

Not because they could stop her.

Because stopping her would be a political act.

The warden bowed.

“Regent,” he said, “the King has not—”

“He doesn’t need to authorize a petition,” Gao Yue replied. “He only needs to receive it.”

She held up a scroll.

At its top was the circle stamp.

Center.

Below it were thousands of thumbprints.

Real.

Messy.

A crowd turned into ink.

“We have signatures,” Gao Yue said. “The hungry ask for stability. The frightened ask for walls. The grieving ask for meaning.”

The warden’s mouth tightened.

“And you bring them an answer.”

Gao Yue’s smile was small.

“I bring them a crown,” she said.

## Scene 2

- **POV**: The Spirit King
- **Location**: Behind the palace screens
- **Goal**: Refuse capture by narrative; decide what kind of center to be

He heard them before he saw them.

Not footsteps.

Intention.

A procession carried by a thousand small fears.

He could see branches.

In some, he refused them outright.

Civil war intensified.

The nobles blamed him.

The Ten Sects splintered.

Hellborne factions built their own courts in the ashes.

In other branches, he accepted.

The court hardened.

The registry became a chain.

Peace lasted longer.

But it was a peace that tasted like obedience.

He exhaled.

Every future was a trade.

He watched Lady Gao Yue’s face through the screen.

Her eyes were calm.

Her hands were clean.

He thought of the Rite of Clean Hands.

He thought of the Name-Eater.

He thought of how quickly “order” became “ownership.”

He stepped forward.

The wardens opened the inner gate.

The procession fell silent as if the air itself had been commanded.

Lady Gao Yue bowed.

Not low.

Low enough to look respectful.

High enough to look like a partner.

“Your Majesty,” she said.

He did not correct her.

Correction would be a political act.

She lifted the petition.

“The realm is breaking,” she continued. “Ten sect leaders strike at Houses. Hellborne factions seize infrastructure. The hungry riot. The canals clog with bodies.”

Her voice remained even.

“People beg for a center,” she said. “We offer one. A court under your name.”

He looked at the petition.

Thumbprints.

A crowd turned into consent.

Consent turned into a weapon.

“Why do you want my name?” he asked.

Gao Yue’s smile didn’t change.

“Because names bind,” she said.

He felt the realm shiver.

She had said it like a prayer.

Like a confession.

Like a threat.

## Scene 3

- **POV**: Lady Qiu Ren
- **Location**: Rooftops near the palace district
- **Goal**: Stop the nobles from claiming the King; prevent the court from hardening

Ren could feel her name still bruised.

Even after the escape.

Even after burning every roster she could find.

The court’s reach had left fingerprints on her spirit.

She watched the palace steps from a roofline.

White banners.

White cloth.

White petitions.

A procession that looked like peace.

Ren spat.

Peace was what the powerful called it when the knives were hidden.

A Seven Sword courier crouched beside her.

“They’re petitioning,” he hissed. “To make the King the Compact’s crown.”

Ren’s stomach dropped.

If the Spirit King became a symbol the Houses could wield, the Ten Sects would be fighting not just nobles.

They would be fighting *legitimacy*.

Ren’s grip tightened on her spear.

“We can’t let them do it,” she whispered.

The courier’s eyes were hard.

“And if the King agrees?”

Ren’s throat tightened.

Then the realm would choose a cage.

Because cages were easier to build than trust.

Ren looked toward the palace.

She saw, at the edge of the steps, a lacquer box carried by guards.

She recognized the posture of people carrying something dangerous.

Something sacred.

Something irreversible.

Ren’s blood ran cold.

Whatever the nobles planned, they were no longer escalating in the streets.

They were escalating at the center.

She rose.

“Send word,” she told the courier. “All vice leaders. Now. The palace is the battlefield.”

Below, the crowd began to chant.

“WALLS HOLD. NAMES BIND.”

The chant climbed the steps like a tide.

And Ren understood: the nobles had found the final escalation.

They were trying to turn the realm’s god into their seal.
