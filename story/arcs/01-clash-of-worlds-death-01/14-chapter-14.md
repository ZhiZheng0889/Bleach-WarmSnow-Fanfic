## Scene 1

- **POV**: A crowd-listener (unregistered laborer)
- **Location**: The public square before the white court
- **Goal**: Decide what kind of safety to believe in

The square had become a mouth.

It swallowed people.

It swallowed noise.

It swallowed the small private thoughts that kept a person from turning into a crowd.

White banners hung from pillars.

The marble basin sat at the center like a promise.

Guards stood in straight lines.

Not to protect.

To demonstrate that protection existed.

The laborer—Jian, no surname, because he’d kept his hands off the Registry as long as he could—stood near the canal edge where he could run if running became necessary.

He had watched the city’s rules change in a week.

First seals.

Then edicts.

Then curfews.

Then marks.

Then that strange pressure at night, when names felt heavy in the throat.

The nobles called it order.

The sects called it a cage.

The Cinderbound called it an opportunity.

Jian called it hunger, wearing a new uniform.

A herald shouted.

“In the name of the Marble Compact!”

The crowd answered with a chant that had learned how to travel.

“WALLS HOLD. NAMES BIND.”

Jian felt his stomach twist.

He had repeated it once, days ago, because he wanted flour.

The words had tasted like ash.

A priest raised his hands.

The bells rang.

The Rite of Clean Hands began again, faster than last time, as if ritual were a machine you could crank harder when the world threatened to slip.

And then the air changed.

Not like a spell.

Like weather.

The crowd quieted—not because it was instructed, but because something in the square made silence feel appropriate.

The palace screens parted.

A figure stepped out.

No crown.

No weapons.

No banner.

Just presence.

The Spirit King.

Jian had never seen him before.

He had heard his name used like a tool.

Now he saw the man behind it.

The priest’s face froze mid-smile.

Lady Gao Yue stood at the steps, expression composed, posture perfect.

The crowd’s breathing synchronized without anyone asking.

Jian hated that most of all.

How easily bodies agreed.

The Spirit King looked over them.

Not like a ruler inspecting.

Like someone counting exits.

He spoke.

“I was asked to crown a compact,” he said.

A ripple ran through the square.

Priests stiffened.

Guards tightened their grip.

Lady Gao Yue did not move.

“I will not,” the Spirit King continued.

Silence.

Sharp as glass.

Jian felt the crowd’s fear wobble, searching for a new shape.

Then the Spirit King lifted his gaze.

“I will not,” he repeated, “because a crown makes a lie sound permanent.”

A priest found his voice.

“Your Majesty—”

The Spirit King held up a hand.

The gesture was small.

The effect was not.

The priest stopped as if language itself had been interrupted.

Jian swallowed.

So this was what power looked like when it didn’t need a script.

The Spirit King’s voice softened.

“You are hungry,” he said.

The words were plain.

No myth.

No doctrine.

Just the one truth everyone shared.

Jian felt his eyes sting.

He hated himself for it.

The Spirit King continued.

“You are afraid,” he said.

Heads dipped.

Not in reverence.

In recognition.

“And you are being offered safety in exchange for obedience,” he said.

A murmur stirred.

Lady Gao Yue’s eyes narrowed by a fraction.

The Spirit King did not look at her.

He looked at the crowd.

“Obedience can keep you alive,” he said. “It can also keep you owned.”

The murmurs grew.

Jian felt the square shift under his feet.

The crowd wanted to agree.

The crowd also wanted bread.

The Spirit King breathed in, slow.

“I will not tell you to refuse the seals,” he said.

The priest blinked.

Lady Gao Yue’s posture loosened, just barely.

A safer speech.

A manageable speech.

Then the Spirit King broke it.

“I will tell you this,” he said. “If someone must count you in order to protect you, then you must demand the right to count them back.”

The words hit like a thrown stone.

Count them back.

Jian felt heat rise behind his ribs.

A guard near the front shifted.

A clerk’s face went pale.

Lady Gao Yue’s calm held, but it was now the calm of a person watching a fire approach her house.

The Spirit King raised his voice slightly.

“Ask where the grain comes from,” he said. “Ask who decides the marks. Ask who writes the names into the scrolls.”

Jian heard someone whisper, “Scrolls.”

He tasted fear again.

The Name-Eater.

The Spirit King’s voice did not change.

“Ask,” he said, “why a system needs you to be smaller in order to be safe.”

The priest’s hands trembled.

A chant tried to rise—familiar, rehearsed.

“WALLS HOLD—”

The Spirit King spoke over it.

“Walls hold,” he agreed.

The crowd faltered.

The chant broke.

Jian’s breath caught.

If the King could take their words and twist them, then nobody owned the language anymore.

“Walls hold,” the Spirit King repeated. “But walls also trap smoke.”

A low sound ran through the crowd.

Not approval.

Not dissent.

Understanding.

“And names bind,” he continued. “But names can be used like rope.”

Lady Gao Yue took one step forward.

Careful.

Respectful.

A court step.

“Your Majesty,” she said, voice clear, “we only want stability.”

The Spirit King finally looked at her.

His gaze was not angry.

That was worse.

It was tired.

“Stability for whom?” he asked.

Lady Gao Yue’s smile sharpened.

“For everyone,” she said.

The Spirit King’s voice softened again.

“Then release the marks,” he said. “Release the name-binding. Feed the unregistered without punishment.”

Lady Gao Yue’s smile did not move.

Because now the choice was public.

And she could not choose mercy without losing control.

Jian felt the crowd lean forward.

Waiting.

Lady Gao Yue bowed.

“A realm cannot be governed by ideals,” she said.

The Spirit King nodded.

“And a realm cannot be saved by chains,” he replied.

The square held its breath.

Then—without instruction—someone near the back began a different chant.

Not the court’s.

Not the sects’.

Just a question turned into rhythm.

“WHO DECIDES?”

Jian felt his skin prickle.

It spread.

“WHO DECIDES?”

The priest looked panicked.

The guards looked uncertain.

Lady Gao Yue’s eyes went flat.

The Spirit King closed his eyes for a heartbeat.

As if he had just thrown a match and accepted that the fire would not ask permission before it spread.

## Scene 2

- **POV**: Lady Gao Yue
- **Location**: The steps above the square
- **Goal**: Salvage the narrative; turn the speech into proof the Compact is necessary

A speech could be corrected.

That was the lie Gao Yue told herself as the crowd’s chant shifted.

“WHO DECIDES?”

It wasn’t a riot yet.

It was worse.

It was a thought that could travel.

Gao Yue signaled with two fingers.

Her spymaster moved in the crowd, invisible as habit.

Clerks positioned themselves near the ration desks.

Guards tightened their line.

Not to strike.

To remind.

Gao Yue raised her voice.

“People of the realm,” she called.

The chant stumbled.

Some heads turned.

Enough.

“We decide together,” she said.

A beautiful lie.

She continued.

“The Compact is your answer,” she said. “Your ration halls. Your patrols. Your bridges kept open.”

She gestured toward the basin.

“Clean hands,” she said. “A clean system. A promise that you will not be devoured by mobs or bandits.”

She did not say *Hellborne*.

She did not say *Ten Sects*.

Naming enemies too clearly gave them form.

Better to leave them as shadows.

The Spirit King watched her.

She felt his gaze like a weight.

Gao Yue kept smiling.

“Your Majesty has offered questions,” she said, turning his words into a harmless philosophy lesson.

Then she sharpened the blade.

“We offer bread,” she added.

A murmur of agreement moved through the front ranks.

Hunger was still the oldest listener.

Gao Yue lifted her hand.

“Return to the registry halls,” she said. “Take your seals. Keep your families safe.”

She heard the crowd’s question chant falter.

Good.

Then she heard, from the canal edge, a different rhythm rising.

Not “WHO DECIDES?”

A response.

“WE DO.”

Gao Yue’s smile tightened.

So the speech had behaved exactly as speeches always behaved:

It had escaped.

And now she would have to escalate again.

Not with swords.

With policy.

With marks.

With the slow, patient violence of administration.

She leaned toward her priest, voice low.

“Draft a new rite,” she whispered.

The priest swallowed.

“What kind?”

Gao Yue’s eyes stayed on the crowd.

“One that makes questions feel sinful,” she said.

## Scene 3

- **POV**: Lady Qiu Ren
- **Location**: The crowd’s edge, near the canal
- **Goal**: Pull the speech into action; protect the beginning of a counter-seal network

Ren had not expected the King to speak like a person.

She had expected myth.

She had expected holy distance.

Instead he spoke of hunger.

And the crowd listened.

Ren felt her name bruise again as she moved through bodies.

Not the Name-Eater this time.

Just the old ache of being known.

A Seven Sword vice leader slipped beside her.

“He just started a revolt,” Yan muttered.

Ren’s eyes stayed on the ration desks.

“No,” she said. “He started a vocabulary.”

Yan’s jaw clenched.

“And now the nobles will make it illegal.”

Ren nodded.

“Yes,” she said.

She watched Lady Gao Yue’s hands.

Watched her gestures.

Watched the way guards shifted at the same time.

A system responding like a single body.

Ren turned away.

“We can’t win this square,” she whispered.

Yan bristled.

“Then why are we here?”

Ren touched the pouch at her belt.

Inside was a small strip of paper.

Not a ration seal.

Not a Bandit Mark.

A different stamp.

A counter-seal: a simple symbol burned into fiber with a heated coin.

It meant one thing.

*Help.*

No law.

No oath.

Just a promise that someone would meet you at night with medicine or food or a route out of the city.

Ren had only a handful.

But handfuls were how revolutions began.

The Mother Trinity vice leader appeared at her other side, eyes exhausted.

“People are already asking for medicine,” he said.

Ren nodded.

“Give it,” she replied. “No questions. No registry.”

Yan grimaced.

“And when the Compact arrests us?”

Ren looked at the crowd.

At the chant splitting into arguments.

At the arguments splitting into choices.

“At least,” she said, “the hungry will remember there was another way to be protected.”

She slipped into an alley.

Behind her, the square roared.

Ahead of her, the city waited.

And somewhere, in the furnace districts, an infrastructure older than courts was already preparing to answer.
