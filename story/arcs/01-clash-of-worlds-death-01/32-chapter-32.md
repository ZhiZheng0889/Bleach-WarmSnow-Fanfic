## Scene 1

- **POV**: Lady Gao Yue
- **Location**: A reopened ration point beneath white banners
- **Goal**: Turn hunger into obedience; make refusal look like crime

The line was a spine.

It ran through the street with the stiff dignity of people pretending not to shake.

White banners hung above them.

Not as comfort.

As instruction.

Lady Gao Yue watched from a raised platform, her hands folded like a seal that had not yet pressed.

The ration point had been reopened under strict Audit.

Strict meant slow.

Slow meant angry.

Angry meant useful.

A clerk read names.

A guard checked wrists for wax stamps.

A second clerk checked the first clerk.

A third clerk checked the ink.

No one trusted anyone.

That was the Compact’s true religion.

A woman stepped forward.

Her hands were empty.

Her eyes were full.

“Name,” the clerk demanded.

The woman said it.

The clerk frowned.

“That name is crossed,” he said.

“I’m standing here,” the woman replied.

“That proves nothing,” the clerk said.

The guard lifted his baton.

Lady Gao Yue did not move.

She let the moment breathe.

If she intervened, she would have to admit the system had a heart.

She would not.

The baton struck.

Not hard.

Just enough.

A soft sound.

A loud lesson.

The line tightened.

Bodies did not step back.

They stepped inward.

Inward was crowd.

Crowd was heat.

Heat was riot.

A boy shouted.

“Thieves!”

He did not mean the woman.

He meant the clerks.

He meant the banners.

He meant the word Audit.

Lady Gao Yue’s mouth became a calm curve.

“Proceed,” she said.

The clerk swallowed and read another name.

The line surged.

A hand reached.

A sack tore.

Grain spilled onto stone like pale teeth.

The guard swung again.

Someone swung back.

Not with a baton.

With a ladle.

It clanged off helmet.

It sounded like a bell for the end of manners.

Lady Gao Yue raised two fingers.

Enforcers moved.

They wore white cloth over steel.

They did not look like soldiers.

They looked like paperwork that had learned to walk.

“Banditry,” she said, softly.

The word was a net.

Once spoken, anyone could be caught.

She leaned toward her steward.

“Announce it,” she whispered.

He hesitated.

“Even them?”

Lady Gao Yue’s eyes stayed on the line.

“Especially them,” she said.

Because the Compact did not fear the Cinderbound today.

It feared the line learning it could move.

## Scene 2

- **POV**: Ren
- **Location**: A back-room kitchen behind a laundry house
- **Goal**: Feed without being counted; teach a new kind of order

Ren listened to the city’s stomach.

It had a language.

It spoke in footsteps.

In the scrape of bowls.

In the tiny coughs of children who had learned to cough quietly.

The kitchen was not official.

That was why it worked.

A pot simmered.

Not with enough.

Enough was a myth.

But with something.

Something was a beginning.

A woman arrived with bruised wrists.

“Ration point,” she said.

Ren nodded.

He did not ask for details.

Details became stories.

Stories became evidence.

Evidence became a door kicked in at dawn.

He handed her a bowl.

She took it like a secret.

“Your name?” a child asked.

Ren smiled.

“What for?” he asked.

“So we can thank you,” the child said.

Ren shook his head.

“Thank the pot,” he replied. “Thank the hands. Thank the room.”

The child frowned.

“That’s not a person,” she said.

Ren stirred the broth.

“That’s the point,” he said.

A runner slipped in.

Her hair smelled like smoke.

“The Compact called it banditry,” she said.

Ren’s eyes narrowed.

“Of course,” he replied.

A steward’s word could make a meal illegal.

A clerk’s ink could turn hunger into crime.

“That’s going to spread,” the runner warned.

Ren nodded again.

“Let it,” he said.

The runner stared.

“They’ll come for us,” she whispered.

Ren lowered his voice.

“Then don’t be a place,” he said.

He tapped the table.

“Be a habit.”

A habit could move.

A habit could hide.

A habit could survive being called a crime.

Outside, the city’s mouth had changed again.

It was learning new words.

Not prayers.

Not slogans.

Instructions.

Share.

Hide.

Run.

Lie.

Live.

## Scene 3

- **POV**: The Spirit King
- **Location**: Where ledgers become weather
- **Goal**: Understand that control and revolt are both blades; feel the seam thinning

He felt the Audit tighten.

It was not paper.

It was hunger arranged into columns.

A neat cruelty.

The Compact believed that if a thing could be counted, it could be owned.

The Cinderbound believed that if a thing could be broken, it could be freed.

Ren believed that if a thing could be repeated, it could be changed.

Three beliefs.

Three knives.

He had been turning friction into misbehavior.

Small failures.

Quiet rebellions.

Tools that refused to obey a single hand.

It had worked.

Until the city began to move like a single body.

Bodies moved.

But worlds moved too.

When enough mouths opened at once, the boundary between life and death listened.

He felt it.

A thinness.

Not in stone.

In meaning.

Names burned.

Lists erased.

Stamps rejected.

When governance lost its language, the dead noticed.

Because the dead were made of language.

Unpaid vows.

Unfinished names.

He tasted something like a draft through a door that should not exist.

It smelled like ash.

Not registry ash.

Older ash.

The kind that came from a place where hunger had no body.

He closed his eyes.

The first clash was still a city problem.

But it was beginning to echo.

And echoes did not stay in the room.
